⎙ Client summary

Method — three layers, kept separate

Measured figures come directly from a published dataset and are cited. Modelled figures are derived by inference or spatial join; each carries its assumption chain and a confidence label. Judgement inputs are planner assertions, marked as such — none are applied yet. No blended score exists: a route gets target boardings (volume) and an audience index (concentration), never one number.

How the audience builder scores

Each subzone's share of your selected audience (age bands × sex, housing groups) is indexed against the national share (100 = national average). Context layers (planning-area geometry) multiply in by geometric mean. Stops inherit their subzone's index; a route's target boardings = Σ allocated stop boardings × index. Combined levers are treated as independent, which overstates concentration where they correlate — multi-lever audiences are directional.

Sources

    Stated assumptions

      Confidence labels

      Subzone levers start at medium; adding a planning-area context layer moves to low. The label widens when less than 80% of a route's allocated volume has audience data, or when more than 10% comes from index-capped subzones. Nothing is labelled better than medium: the residence join (boarders ≈ catchment residents) is unverified and applies to every figure. With no levers selected the ranking is pure measured volume ("everyone").

      Consumer behaviour levers

      Each behaviour lever is a stop-catchment density index: the count of relevant places (supermarkets, hawker centres, pre-schools, pharmacies…) within 400 m of a stop, indexed to the volume-weighted network average. Locations come from cited registries and are observed; the behaviour is inferred — being near a supermarket is evidence of grocery-shopper presence, not proof of shopping. These levers score at stop level (finer than subzone), always carry at most low confidence, and use an additive-smoothed density ratio (count+1 over baseline+1), floored at 25 and capped at 300, so sparse layers grade smoothly instead of flipping between floor and cap. A spend-propensity join from the Household Expenditure Survey 2023 (by dwelling type) has been identified but its by-dwelling tables are not yet machine-readable on data.gov.sg; it is the named next data source, not silently proxied.

      Persona presets

      The persona row mirrors MooveSMART's 16-persona taxonomy for comparability, but each preset is a transparent recipe over the open-data levers below it — hover a persona to see its ingredients. Notable mappings: Property Owner is renamed Property Intender (mover/intender behaviour is what the data supports); Car Owner uses vehicle purchase and running-cost spend and is best used for external formats; Gaming rides on a small spend base (~$3/month household average) — treat as directional. Job Seekers is deliberately not scored: no open-data signal exists (MOM unemployment is national-only), and scoring it would be assertion dressed as data.

      Spend & digital propensity

      Spend levers apply HES 2023 average monthly household expenditure (by goods/services division × dwelling type, SingStat Tablebuilder table 17971) to each subzone's housing mix. The online-shoppers lever applies IMDA's online-shopper rate by age (latest machine-readable year is 2018 — the age gradient is the signal; absolute levels are outdated) to each subzone's age mix. Both are ecological joins: they assume the national rate holds within every dwelling type or age band, so they rank areas by composition, not by observed local behaviour. Because they derive from the Housing and Age levers, the UI clears the source lever when a propensity chip is selected (and vice versa) — stacking them would double-count one dimension.

      External (street-level) model

      Bus exteriors reach a different audience: people waiting at stops, pedestrians, street-level activity along the corridor. The external model resamples each route every ~100 m and composes three observed inputs — corridor tap-ins (all stops within 150 m, any service), street-activity places within 150 m, and the corridor's crossed subzones (which carry your composed audience) — into a relative exposure index (100 = network average). The ranking accumulates that density over route length (exposure-km): a wrapped bus is seen along its whole run, so a long route through active corridors accumulates more exterior exposure than a short dense one — both the per-km density and the accumulated figure are shown. No pedestrian-count dataset exists for Singapore in open data, so this is a ranking signal, never a people count; it carries at most low confidence, and it is never summed with internal figures. Known under-reads: vehicular exposure (no open link-level traffic counts — expressway-heavy routes are under-scored) and behaviour levers (stop-level, not applied to corridors). Monsoon rain (NE Dec–early Mar, SW Jun–Sep) reduces street dwell and visibility — shown as an advisory, deliberately not applied to figures.

      Ethnicity

      Ethnic composition is shown per route and subzone as creative and language guidance only — festive timing, language mix. It is not a selectable audience filter and enters no score, by design.

      Resonance

      Not scored — it cannot be derived from open data. It is measured post-campaign (brand-lift vs matched control), never asserted pre-campaign.
